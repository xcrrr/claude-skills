# Pitch Deck Writer

## Overview
This skill guides the creation of compelling investor pitch decks—from the essential 10-slide structure through the storytelling arc, investor hook, and slide-by-slide narrative. A great pitch deck doesn't just present information; it tells a story that makes investors feel the urgency of the problem, believe in the solution, and trust the team. This skill provides both the structure and the language to achieve that.

## When to Use
- Preparing a deck for a VC, angel investor, or accelerator application
- Structuring a fundraising narrative for a seed, Series A, or growth round
- Writing the narrative/speaker notes for each slide
- Reviewing an existing deck for story flow and missing elements
- Creating a teaser deck (5-slide version) for initial investor outreach

## When NOT to Use
- Writing a 30-page business plan (different format, different audience)
- Creating financial models or detailed projections (use a finance skill)
- Building the actual slide design/visuals (use a design tool like Canva or Figma)
- Writing a pitch for non-investor audiences (board decks, sales pitches use different structures)

## Quick Reference
| Slide # | Slide Name | Purpose | Key Element |
|---------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Cover | First impression | Company name, tagline, logo |
| 2 | Problem | Create urgency | 1 sharp pain point + data evidence |
| 3 | Solution | The "aha" moment | Clear value prop, how it works |
| 4 | Market | Size the opportunity | TAM/SAM/SOM + why now |
| 5 | Product | Make it real | Screenshot, demo, key features |
| 6 | Traction | Prove momentum | Revenue, growth, retention, logos |
| 7 | Business Model | Show how you make money | Revenue streams, unit economics |
| 8 | Competition | Differentiate | Positioning matrix (2×2) or feature table |
| 9 | Team | Build confidence | Why this team wins |
| 10 | The Ask | Close the deal | Amount, use of funds, milestones |

## Instructions

### Step 1: The Investor Hook — The First 60 Seconds
Investors see hundreds of decks. You have 60 seconds to make them lean in. Your opening must do three things:
1. State a surprising or provocative fact about the problem
2. Make investors feel the pain viscerally
3. Hint that you have a fundamentally different solution

**Hook formula:** "[Shocking fact about the problem]. [Current terrible solution]. We built [product] that [core benefit] for [target customer]."

**Example:** "Every year, $40 billion in legal fees goes to attorneys doing work that AI can do in seconds. Law firms are still using Word and email to review contracts. We built ContractAI—the first AI platform that reviews 200-page contracts in 90 seconds, at 1% of the attorney cost."

### Step 2: Slide-by-Slide Narrative

**Slide 1: Cover**
- Company name and logo
- One-line tagline that conveys the value proposition: "[Product] is the [category] for [audience] that [key benefit]"
- Your name and contact info
- Round being raised (optional)

**Slide 2: Problem**
Make the pain vivid and urgent. Use data to prove it's real.
```
Structure:
- Headline: "[X people/companies] suffer from [problem] and lose [cost/time]"
- 3 bullet points or a short story illustrating the pain
- A data point: "Market research shows X% of [target] consider this their #1 challenge"
- The current alternatives and why they fail

Do NOT: List 5 problems. Pick ONE and make it devastating.
```

**Slide 3: Solution**
The "aha" slide. Make the solution feel obvious in retrospect.
```
Structure:
- Headline: "Introducing [Product]: [one-line description]"
- How it works in 3 steps (not a feature list!)
- 1–2 screenshots or product visuals
- The key insight that makes your approach work
```

**Slide 4: Market Size**
Show this is worth investors' time and capital.
```
Structure:
- TAM: Total addressable market (use credible analyst sources)
- SAM: Your serviceable market (apply realistic filters)
- SOM: What you'll capture in 3–5 years
- "Why now" box: 2–3 tailwinds that make this moment special
  (regulatory change, new technology, behavioral shift)
```

**Slide 5: Product**
Make it real. Show, don't tell.
```
Structure:
- Hero screenshot or 30-second demo GIF
- 3 key features (not 10) with one-line benefit statements
- A customer quote or testimonial if available
- "Coming soon" roadmap item (shows vision without overpromising)
```

**Slide 6: Traction**
This is the most important slide after Series A. Show momentum.
```
Priority metrics (pick 2–3 that look best):
- Revenue (MRR or ARR) + growth rate
- User/customer growth (especially if viral)
- Retention / NRR (Net Revenue Retention)
- Logos of notable customers
- "Signed LOIs from 5 enterprise customers" if pre-revenue

Best chart: Revenue graph with a sharp hockey-stick curve.
Annotate inflection points: "Launched X → growth accelerated"
```

**Slide 7: Business Model**
Explain how you make money clearly and quickly.
```
Structure:
- Revenue model: subscription, usage, marketplace, etc.
- Pricing tiers (3 tiers typical: Starter / Growth / Enterprise)
- Unit economics:
  - CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
  - LTV (Lifetime Value)
  - LTV:CAC ratio (target > 3:1)
  - Payback period (target < 18 months for SaaS)
- Path to profitability (when do the unit economics become favorable?)
```

**Slide 8: Competition**
Don't say "we have no competition." Show you understand the landscape.
```
Two formats:
1. Positioning matrix (2×2):
   - X-axis: Price (budget → premium)
   - Y-axis: Key differentiator (e.g., AI-native → manual)
   - Your logo is top-right (or wherever the whitespace is)

2. Feature comparison table:
   | Feature | Us | Comp A | Comp B |
   |---------|----|----|---|
   | Feature 1 | ✅ | ✅ | ❌ |
   | Our key differentiator | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ |

Differentiation tagline: "Unlike [competitor], we [key differentiator] which means [customer benefit]"
```

**Slide 9: Team**
VCs bet on people as much as ideas—especially early stage.
```
Structure:
- Founder photos and names
- 1–2 line bio focusing on domain expertise and relevant past wins
- Why this team? The "unfair advantage" story
  - "10 years at the largest law firm in the US before founding this"
  - "Previously built and sold a legal tech company to Thomson Reuters"
- Notable advisors (with logos of relevant companies)
- Key hires needed (honest signal of self-awareness)
```

**Slide 10: The Ask**
Be specific. Vague asks signal unpreparedness.
```
Structure:
- Amount raising: "$4M Seed Round"
- Round structure: SAFE / priced equity / convertible note
- Use of funds (pie chart or 3-line breakdown):
  - 50% → Engineering (3 senior engineers)
  - 30% → Sales & Marketing (first dedicated GTM hire)
  - 20% → Operations & runway
- Milestones this round achieves:
  - "Reach $1M ARR by Q4 2025"
  - "Launch enterprise tier and sign 5 enterprise customers"
  - "18 months runway to Series A"
- Call to action: "We'd love to set up a follow-up call this week"
```

### Step 3: The Storytelling Arc
The best pitch decks follow a hero's journey narrative:
1. **World before** (Problem slide): Status quo is broken
2. **The disruption** (Market/Solution): Why now, why this approach
3. **The hero emerges** (Team + Product): We are uniquely positioned to solve this
4. **Evidence of victory** (Traction): We are already winning
5. **The journey ahead** (Ask + Milestones): Join us to complete the mission

### Step 4: Teaser Deck (5 slides for cold outreach)
For email attachments or first contact, condense to 5 slides:
1. Cover + one-line problem/solution
2. Problem + traction in one slide
3. Solution + product screenshot
4. Market size + why now
5. Team + the ask

## Examples

### Example 1: Series A Pitch Deck Outline

**Company:** TaskFlow — AI-powered project management for software teams

**Slide narrative flow:**

**Slide 2 — Problem:**
> "Software teams lose 30% of their week to status updates, meetings, and chasing down blockers. A Stripe engineering team with 20 engineers wastes $1.8M/year in productive time just coordinating work. Every existing tool—Jira, Asana, Linear—makes engineers fill in data; none of them generate it automatically."

**Slide 3 — Solution:**
> "TaskFlow connects to GitHub, Slack, and your calendar. It reads what your team is actually doing and automatically generates standup summaries, updates ticket statuses, and surfaces blockers—before your manager has to ask."

**Slide 6 — Traction:**
> "$280K ARR | 3× growth in 6 months | 94% 12-month retention | 22 paying teams including Vercel, Notion, and Replit"

**Slide 10 — Ask:**
> "Raising $5M Series A on a SAFE. Use of funds: 60% engineering (4 hires), 25% sales (first AE + SDR), 15% ops. Milestones: $1.5M ARR, 5 enterprise customers, launch Jira migration tool."

### Example 2: Slide-by-Slide Narrative for a Fintech Seed Deck

**Company:** ClearPay — BNPL for dental practices

**Cover tagline:** "ClearPay: Helping dental patients afford the care they need—and helping practices grow revenue 40%."

**Problem slide copy:**
> Headline: "47 million Americans skip dental care every year because they can't afford it—even with insurance."
>
> - The average American dental bill: $1,800 out of pocket
> - 68% of patients decline recommended treatment when quoted the full cost upfront
> - Dental practices lose $380K/year in declined treatment
> - CareCredit, the #1 dental financing option, has a 37% approval rejection rate—leaving practices with no alternative to offer

**Solution slide copy:**
> Headline: "ClearPay gives every patient a payment plan in 60 seconds—no credit score required."
>
> How it works:
> 1. Patient gets treatment quote at front desk
> 2. ClearPay app shows approved payment plan in 60 seconds (income-based underwriting)
> 3. Practice gets paid in full within 48 hours; patient pays ClearPay over 6–24 months

**Traction slide copy:**
> "Launched 8 months ago. $120K MRR, growing 25% month-over-month. 140 dental practices. 4,200 patient payment plans issued. 0% default rate to date. Waitlist of 300 practices."

## Best Practices
- Every slide should have one message—if you need to explain it in presenter notes, the slide is too complex
- Lead with traction if you have it—investors want evidence, not promises
- Use real numbers, not projections, wherever possible; label projections clearly as forecasts
- Anticipate the top 3 investor objections and address them proactively in your slides
- Keep total slide count to 10–15; appendix slides are fine for deep-dive questions
- Get feedback from at least 3 people who have seen hundreds of decks before finalizing
- Send the deck as a PDF, not a link to Notion or Google Slides, for cold outreach

## Common Mistakes
- "We have no competition" — this destroys credibility instantly
- Burying traction in slide 9 when it should be slide 2 or 3 (if strong)
- Market slides that show only TAM with no path to SOM
- Team slides that list job titles but not why those people win this specific market
- Financial projections that go "up and to the right" without explaining the model assumptions
- Designing the deck before writing the narrative — story first, design second
- 11-point font to fit everything on one slide — cut content instead

## Tips & Tricks
- The "So what?" test: read each slide and ask "so what?" — if the answer isn't obvious, rewrite the headline to make the conclusion explicit
- VCs share decks internally; make sure every slide is comprehensible without your voice-over
- Benchmark your traction against public SaaS benchmarks (e.g., "our NRR of 115% is above the median for Series A SaaS")
- End your pitch meeting by asking: "What would you need to see to feel comfortable making an investment?" — this surfaces hidden objections
- The appendix is free space: add deep technical architecture, detailed financials, cohort analysis, and customer case studies there
